# Barbells vs Machines for Hypertrophy

**Date:** 2026-03-02
**Source:** [@DeanTTraining](https://x.com/DeanTTraining/status/2027870713047052737)

## The Argument

Dean Turner's take: drop barbell bench press, squat, and deadlift. Replace with:

- **Flat Chest Press Machine** (replaces barbell bench)
- **Pendulum / Hack Squat** (replaces barbell squat)
- **45-Degree Back Extension** (replaces barbell deadlift)

Core claim: for building your best physique, machines are the better tool.

## Discussion

### Why This Makes Sense

**For hypertrophy specifically**, the muscle doesn't care what's loading it. What matters is:
- Tension through the full range of motion
- Progressive overload over time
- Taking sets close to failure safely

Machines excel at all three. A chest press machine keeps constant tension on the pecs through the entire ROM — no lockout rest at the top like barbell bench. A hack squat loads the quads without the limiting factor of core stability or balance. You can push closer to true failure on a machine because there's no risk of getting pinned.

**Barbell compounds are overrated for hypertrophy** because:
- The stabilizer demand limits how much you can load the target muscle
- Barbell bench fatigues your shoulders and triceps before your chest is truly done
- Barbell squats are limited by lower back and core fatigue, not quad capacity
- Conventional deadlifts are a full-body tax — great for strength, mediocre for targeting any single muscle group

### Why Barbells Still Have Value

- **Functional strength and movement patterns** — squatting and hinging under load transfers to real life
- **Bone density and CNS adaptation** — heavy axial loading (barbell on back) is uniquely effective
- **Ego / fun** — let's be honest, hitting a barbell PR just feels different
- **Efficiency** — one exercise, many muscles, less time

### The Practical Angle (Arrilaga)

This advice is especially relevant for training at a crowded gym:
- Barbells require a rack, which are the most contested equipment
- Machines are usually more available (multiple stations, faster rotations)
- No spotter needed on machines — can train solo at full intensity
- Station-based grouping is easier when you're not waiting for a rack

## Application to Functional Bulk Program

**Current program uses barbells for:**
- Squat (Day 5 Legs) — could swap to hack squat or leg press as primary
- RDL (Day 5 Legs) — could swap to 45-degree extension or machine hip hinge
- Note: bench press is already DB-based, not barbell

**Recommended swaps if barbells are unavailable:**
| Barbell Exercise | Machine Alternative | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Back Squat | Hack Squat or Leg Press | Hack squat = closer to squat pattern, leg press = more load capacity |
| RDL | 45-Degree Back Extension (weighted) | Not available at Arrilaga — use cable pull-through or DB RDL instead |
| Barbell Bench | Chest Press Machine | Already using DB bench in current program |
| Barbell Row | Seated Cable Row / Chest-Supported Row | Already using cable rows in current program |

**What we DON'T have at Arrilaga:** The 45-degree back extension machine. Alternatives for posterior chain:
- DB Romanian Deadlift (no rack needed)
- Cable Pull-Through
- Lying Leg Curl + Back Extension combo
- Kettlebell Swings (already in KB days)

## Verdict

For the powerbuilder goal (strong + dense, not competing in powerlifting), this is solid advice. The program is already mostly machine/DB-based for upper body. The main question is legs — and honestly, leg press is already the heaviest lift on Day 5. Could easily promote leg press or hack squat to the primary movement and keep barbell squat as an optional "if the rack is free" exercise.

**TL;DR:** Machines are underrated for hypertrophy. Barbells are great but not mandatory. At a crowded gym, being flexible with equipment is more important than dogma about the "big 3."
